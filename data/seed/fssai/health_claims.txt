FSSAI Health and Nutrition Claims — Food Safety and Standards (Advertising and Claims) Regulations, 2018

Section 3.1 — Permissible Health Claims
Health claims must be based on generally accepted scientific evidence and must not attribute to any food the property of preventing, treating, or curing a human disease, nor refer to such properties. Words like "boosts immunity" are permitted only when accompanied by an approved substantiation statement filed with FSSAI; unqualified immunity claims are not permitted.

Section 3.2 — Nutrient Content Claims
Claims such as "0g trans fat", "low sodium", "high in protein" are permitted only if the product meets the specific quantitative thresholds defined in Schedule IV (e.g. "0g trans fat" requires less than 0.5g trans fat per serving, and the product must not simultaneously be high in saturated fat without disclosure).

Section 3.3 — Disease Risk Reduction Claims
Claims implying reduction of disease risk (e.g. "reduces risk of heart disease") are prohibited on food products without prior specific FSSAI approval under a separate notified procedure.

Section 3.4 — Claims Targeting Children
Any health or nutrition claim on a product marketed to children must be reviewed against the stricter Schedule VI criteria, which prohibit claims implying medicinal benefit and require simplified, age-appropriate substantiation language.

Section 3.5 — "Natural" and "Ayurvedic" Claims on Food Products
A food product (regulated solely by FSSAI, not jointly with AYUSH) may not use the word "Ayurvedic" or "medicinal" on its packaging or in advertising, as this implies a drug/medicine classification outside FSSAI's mandate.
